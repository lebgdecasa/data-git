# $100M Leads — Bonus Chapters (early draft of "$100M Money Models")

Source: owner-uploaded PDF "100M LEADS SECOND PART" (24 scanned pages, OCR-distilled).
These are Alex Hormozi's bonus chapters on **Promotions: Premium, Free, and Discount** —
the "Attract" section. Promotions are wrappers around a Grand Slam Offer, used to get
people to engage who otherwise wouldn't.

## Origin story (why it matters)

2016: unknown 26-year-old gym owner speaks at a conference, shares exact numbers from
pre-selling his gyms via the Free 6-Week Challenge → gets mobbed → 100+ business cards →
creates the first Gym Launch offer, which was FREE:

> "I'll fly out to your gym. I'll spend my own money on marketing. I'll work your leads.
> I'll close them. I get the cash that I collect, and I'll give you the customers for free.
> I only make what I sell."

33 gyms, 18 months, first $1M outside his own gyms. **Free for the client ≠ unpaid for you.**

## Why start free/discounted in a new market

1. You don't know what you're doing yet — free buys forgiveness while you sharpen.
2. Fastest route to testimonials.
3. Lack of conviction kills early sales; free makes the first closes possible.
4. Free customers refer easily → demand flywheel.

**Get Flow. Monetize Flow. Then Add Friction.** Keep the front-end as light as possible
for as long as possible if you have upsells behind it.

## Premium offers (the Grand Slam Offer sold naked)

The $50,000 burger lesson: to a $25,000/hr earner, a $50k burger costs the same two work-hours
as a $13.50 combo costs a minimum-wage worker. **Price has no ceiling; cost has a floor at $0.**

Math: business A makes $50 profit/sale, business B $9,500 profit/sale. From the same 190 buyers,
converting just 5% to the premium offer yields $90,250 vs $9,500 — **9.5x profit, ~10 clients
instead of 190.**

**Pros:** simplest math, fewest moving parts · highest-quality customers (less operational drag) ·
transparent intentions, high lead quality.
**Cons:** most expensive cost per lead, slower break-even · demands deep avatar knowledge and
sharp copy (free/discount offers forgive weak copy; premium doesn't) · demands real sales skill ·
least efficient way to capture a marketplace · requires a truly compelling offer (bonuses,
guarantees, premium support).

**Rule:** if starting from zero — start free/discount, prove results, THEN restructure premium.
Premium works beautifully as the SECOND offer after a free offer that demonstrated value.

## Free offers

- **Penny Gap (Dan Ariely):** 9x more people take a free Hershey's kiss than a 1-cent one.
- Free = highest lead volume + lowest cost per lead. Giants run free front-ends: Facebook,
  YouTube, Dropbox, Uber, Netflix, Claire's free piercing, European Wax Center free wax.
- If a free offer flops, diagnosis is one of three: (1) they don't want the thing → change it,
  (2) they don't believe you → answer "why free?", (3) wrong pond → targeting problem.
- The "$100 for $1,000" newspaper test: an offer can be SO good nobody believes it.
  **Every generous offer must answer WHY.**

### The myth, killed with data
Four independent split tests (10 markets each): free vs non-free respondents closed at the
SAME rate and same ticket. The difference: free front-ends cut lead costs ~5x.
"If I had one offer to make or my family would be killed — it would be a free offer.
I'd rather wade through crappy leads than stare at an empty calendar."

### Friction levers (quality dial — use AFTER flow exists)
1. **Qualifications** stated in copy/creative/landing page ("must be 25+, employed, homeowner").
2. **Information requirements** — more fields, heavier asks (phone, income), open-ended > multiple choice.
3. **Number of steps** — each step sheds people; 5-step form ≪ 1-step opt-in.
4. **Forced consumption** — must watch the 40-min video before the CTA appears. Highest-quality filter; use when eyeballs are cheap.
5. **Ad length** — longer ads/copy = fewer but more committed clicks.

Free-money math: $1,000 ads → 500 leads, half qualified (250) BEATS $1,000 → 200 leads,
80% qualified (160). Judge campaigns in dollars, not in how clean the lead list feels.

## Discount offers

Only **massive discounts (50%+)** move people who otherwise wouldn't act. Marginal discounts
(5–25%) just burn margin. Discount a **splinter** of the offer — a piece, not the whole thing.

**Pros:**
1. Compliant lead volume where "free" is legally restricted (regulated industries/countries).
2. Collects some cash (helps liquidate acquisition cost — but never build the model on it).
3. Buyers arrive expecting to spend → salespeople sell with more conviction (tested: the
   close-rate boost is the salesperson's psychology, not the leads).
4. **The Two-Step Sale (biggest benefit):** small purchase → card on file → scheduled follow-up →
   premium upsell. Eliminates no-shows (people show up for what they paid for).
5. Upsells become "use the card on file?" — Amazon one-click logic. Removing the
   take-out-your-card moment measurably lifts conversion.

**Cons:** discounting the core trains customers to wait for sales ("giving away the farm") ·
bargain hoppers (the Groupon disease) — solved by treating discount buyers as QUALIFIED LEADS
for the core offer, not as customers, and structuring the splinter accordingly.

**Example two-step:** $19 heavy-metals test consult → $2,100 ten-week detox plan at the
follow-up appointment. Ad → opt-in → $19 phone close + appointment → value delivered →
second appointment booked → premium sale.

**Pro tip — don't give away expensive time:** restructure first visits so an admin (not the
doctor/expert) handles intake, X-rays, financing pre-approval. The expert appears 5 minutes
("Proprietary 5-Minute Appointment Method"), then the in-depth paid appointment gets booked.
Kills no-show cost AND pre-qualifies.

## Section conclusion (verbatim doctrine)

- Not enough response? Your front end isn't appealing enough — give people who wouldn't
  respond a reason to.
- **Generate Flow → Monetize Flow → Increase Friction. In that order.**
- All businesses capitalize on an **information advantage**: you know the customer's
  problem-journey better than they do. Surface the problems they'll meet next; monetize
  the solutions as upsells. That's the design of a winning acquisition strategy.
